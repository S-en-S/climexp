KNMI Climate Explorer: some examples

Here are some examples how to use the KNMI Climate Explorer: climate, variability and predictability at one station, a poor man's global warming fingerprinting, the standard effects of El Niño. If you have not yet already done so, log in with your e-mail address.

Climate, climate variability and seasonal predictability at one station

 You are going to Tombouctou next August, and would like to know whether to bring an umbrella
  1. Underneath the world map on the page "select time series", select "precipitation" and type "Tomb" (you are not sure about the spelling of the rest of the name) into the field after "containg". Press [Get stations].
  2. This gives a list of eight stations, the fifth one is TOMBOUCTOU MALI (MALI), coordinates: 16.80°N, 3.00°W. The link "more info" reveals that the town has a population of 20000, and is located in a marsh,swamp. Go back and follow the link "get data".
  3. The following page gives a time series of precipitation at Tombouctou from 1897-1996 with a gap at 1915-1921. The second plot is more interesting, it shows that the annual rainfall is concentrated in August, with an average rainfall of 77.65mm and standard deviation 41.85mm (click on "raw data" or scroll down a bit to read off the numbers). This is almost the same as De Bilt (82mm, 43mm). Do take an umbrella.
  4. Clicking on "month" reveals that the August rainfall has a clear decadal trend: the 1970s and 1980s were drier than the average. However, in the 1990s rainfall came back to normal. No clear case to leave the umbrella at home. Go back.
  5. The standard correlations underneath the yearly cycle show that the August rainfall (month 8) is not correlated to El Niño (r=-0.01) or the NAO (r=+0.15). In July, the onset of the rainy season, there could be a connection with El Niño.
  6. If you want to investigate this connection further, select "NINO3" under "system-defined timeseries", and under "common options" select the starting month [Jul] and press [Correlate]. The resulting scatter plot shows that the very wettest years have all been La Niña years, but the two driest years have also been during a La Niña. The tercile plot also shows that there is not much you can say about the chances of a wet/normal/dry July at Tombouctou if you can predict the NINO3 index.
  7. The situation is slightly better if you look at the whole rain season, July-September. Go back to the "common options" and select [averaging] over [3] months and press [Correlate]. With a stromg El Niño at this time of the year (NINO3>+1.25) there were five years with drier than average conditions, and one with wetter than average (1905), so it seems likely it will be drier next time. The tercile plot gives a forecast of 50:30:20 for a NINO3 index of 1.4. During a La Niña the spread is greater, with a 20:40:40 forecast for NINO3 at -1.
  8. Finally, just to make sure, go back to "common options" and change "lag" to [0:24]. The lag correlations show the NINO3-autocorrelation function in reverse; nothing interesting here.
  9. Are there other oceans where the surface temperature was a predictor for Timbouctou rainfall? Go back to the correlations menu, and select "temperature anomalies" under "field: observations". We still have starting month [Jul], [averaging] over [3] months (to cut down the noise), it is wise to also choose lag: [3] months to avoid cause/effect ambiguities. Press [correlate] and wait a while. Besides the ENSO-region, SST in the Mediterranean and North Atlantic stand out. However, these disappear when one takes year-on-year differences ("difference"); so the interannual variability seems (tenuously) connected to ENSO only. The time series are probably not long enough, and the data not homogeneous enough, to say much about the decadal variability. Please send me the preprint if you feel you can do something here.

Poor man's global warming fingerprinting

 What have been the patterns associated with global warming over the past 100 years?
  1. Go to the first page of the KNMI Climate explorer, select from the list of "climate indices" the Jones "global average temperature".
  2. Click on [correlate with a field]
  3. Select under "observations" the Jones "temperature anomalies", and choose to plot the "slope". Under "options" select starting month [Jan], [averaging] over [12] months for yearly averages.
  4. The automatic colour-chosing scheme is suboptimal due to the huge slope at Svelbard (Spitsbergen), go back and set the contours from -2.5 to 2.5.
  5. Now one can see that the world has warmed remarkably uniformly (slope=1). In the Arctic the slope is much larger than one (snow-albedo feedback); and the Pacific decadal pattern and the North Atlantic have been cooling when the rest of the world became warmer. Also the equatorial eastern Pacific has become warmer than the averaged earth. It is not yet known whether these oceanic effects are connected to global warming, or are just coincidences.

The standard effects of El Niño

 What were the effects of El Niño and La Niña over the last hundred years?
  1. Select the NINO3 series in the list of climate indices
  2. (Note that the variability is largest in december, that the correlation with itself is 1.00, and the correlation with the NAO essentially zero in winter)
  3. Select under "field: observations" the CRU Hulme "precipitation anomalies", starting month [all], [averaging] over [3] months (traditional seasons), and press [correlate]. Wait a while.
  4. Note the very strong seasonality in most signals.
  5. Do the same for temperature.
  6. Split up the effects of El Niño (index>0) and La Niña (index<0) if you are in a particularly thorough mood.
  7. Compare the DJF and JJA temperature and precipitation plots with the canonical pictures of the effects of El Niño and La Niña based on the Ropelweski and Halpert articles. Please let me know if you have abstracted the correlation plots to new, improved, canonical pictures. (A year later: I made those plots myself.)
